Cyber AI Agents & Models

Our company builds AI agents for cybersecurity powered by a domain‑specialized model compatible with llama.cpp, Ollama, and LM Studio for efficient on‑prem and edge inference.

These agents inherit deep coverage of NIST CSF 2.0, SP 800‑53, Zero Trust (SP 800‑207), RMF, and FIPS/PQC from training on 530k+ examples across 596 NIST publications, enabling policy‑aware reasoning, control mapping, and standards‑aligned guidance directly inside security workflows.

Deployed as vertical software components, they integrate with CTI platforms using STIX 2.1/TAXII and stream APIs to enrich indicators, TTPs, and knowledge graphs—accelerating triage and threat investigation while preserving traceability to sources.

Our GGUF‑based quantizations (Q4_K_M/Q5_K_M/Q8_0) balance speed and quality on CPU‑first infrastructure, keeping real‑time analysis feasible without dedicated GPUs.

By aligning detection, risk management, and automated response with authoritative NIST guidance and modern TIPs, our agents elevate SIEM and CTI outcomes from raw alerts to actionable, standards‑conformant decisions—supporting security and resilience for critical operations.

INTEGRATIONS

Wazuh + OpenCTI/MISP — Native Integrations for Actionable Intelligence

Our agents plug into Wazuh, the open‑source unified XDR/SIEM, to enhance threat hunting through its built‑in MITRE ATT&CK module, mapping alerts to tactics and techniques and leveraging Wazuh’s file integrity monitoring, configuration assessment, and automated responses.

This alignment provides immediate adversary‑behavior context and faster containment when signals surface on endpoints or servers.

For OpenCTI, we support stream and enrichment connectors so that new indicators and entities create sightings against Wazuh telemetry and remain linked within OpenCTI’s STIX 2.1 knowledge graph. Where appropriate, we use the opencti‑wazuh connector to look up Wazuh events and generate correlated sightings in OpenCTI, while also enabling downstream delivery to SIEMs via OpenCTI’s live streams (e.g., Splunk/Elastic) for continuous indicator matching.

For MISP, we support STIX/TAXII ingestion and the newer TAXII push flow so curated events, tags, and attributes feed detection at scale; we also leverage Wazuh’s integration patterns to check hashes/IOCs from MISP during real‑time alerting. This bi‑directional loop keeps detections current and enriches cases with provenance and confidence metadata.

FEATURES

AI Models & Agents Features

NIST‑grounded expertise, ready on day one
Our agents are powered by Hack‑NIST‑Coder v1.2 (GGUF)—a domain‑specialized LLM fine‑tuned on 530,912 examples across 596 NIST publications, spanning CSF 2.0, SP 800‑53, Zero Trust (SP 800‑207), RMF, FIPS cryptography, and post‑quantum migration guidance. This foundation lets the agents reason with authoritative controls, cite specific sections, and produce policy‑aligned recommendations without external knowledge bases.

Long‑context reasoning for complex SOC & GRC workflows
The model supports up to 32k tokens of context, enabling multi‑document investigations (e.g., alerts + playbooks + standards excerpts) while maintaining citations and control mappings. Teams can paste entire incident timelines or RMF packages and still receive structured, standards‑referenced outputs.

Efficient on‑prem/edge inference (CPU‑first), air‑gap friendly
Delivered in GGUF, the agents run efficiently via llama.cpp, Ollama, and LM Studio with quantizations like Q4_K_M, Q5_K_M, Q8_0, F16 to balance speed and quality. GGUF embeds both tensors and metadata for fast loading and reproducible behavior across executors—ideal for air‑gapped SOCs, ICS/OT networks, and regulated environments.

Zero Trust & CSF 2.0 workflows baked in
Coverage includes CSF 2.0 (GOVERN function) and Zero Trust Architecture (SP 800‑207), enabling the agent to propose control implementations, gap analyses, POA&Ms, and cross‑walks between policies, safeguards, and detection content. This makes audit prep and design reviews faster and traceable to NIST source texts.

SIEM & CTI enrichment using STIX 2.1/TAXII & live streams
Agents speak STIX 2.1 natively and operate with TAXII feeds, OpenCTI live streams, and CSV feeds to automate indicator ingestion, enrichment, and distribution—keeping detections current while preserving provenance, confidence levels, and expiration semantics.

OpenCTI integration — sightings, knowledge graph, and downstream SIEMs
With OpenCTI connectors, new indicators and entities can trigger sightings against telemetry, staying linked in the STIX 2.1 knowledge graph. Stream connectors forward curated intelligence to SIEMs such as Splunk and Elastic, maintaining bi‑directional synchronization for indicator match rules and alerting.

MISP integration — TAXII push, enrichment modules, and STIX conversion
We support MISP TAXII push to publish curated events as STIX bundles and leverage misp‑modules for enrichment (e.g., hashes, domains). This keeps detections fresh across platforms and reduces the lag between threat discovery and operationalization.

Wazuh alignment — ATT&CK mapping, file integrity, configuration assessment, and response
Deployed alongside Wazuh, agents augment its MITRE ATT&CK module by explaining tactics, techniques, mitigations, and references directly in alerts. Combined with Wazuh’s FIM/SCA and automated response capabilities, analysts receive behavior‑centric insights and guided remediation steps.

Indicator lifecycle, confidence, and expiration management
The agents manage indicator lifecycles (creation, enrichment, de‑duplication, expiration) consistent with OpenCTI streams, TAXII collections, and MISP tagging, carrying confidence levels, source attribution, and TLP markings into SIEM matching policies.

Auditability and end‑to‑end traceability
Outputs include citations to NIST controls and standards sections, with clear rationales and references. In regulated environments, this supports audit trails, evidence collection, and control justification for RMF authorizations and CSF‑aligned governance reporting.

Developer‑friendly and portable
Ready‑to‑use snippets (Modelfiles, CLI examples) let engineers evaluate locally and embed agents in pipelines and SOC tooling. Guidance on quantization (start with Q4_K_M for best overall balance) streamlines deployment on commodity hardware across Linux/macOS.

Open content licensing for NIST materials
The model leverages public‑domain NIST publications; the card clarifies permissive usage for research and commercial deployments, simplifying legal review and enterprise adoption.

 

 

Expanded Feature Highlights

Knowledge completeness across NIST corpus
Beyond controls, the training set includes SP 1800 practice guides, interagency reports (IR), and white papers (CSWP)—covering implementation patterns, architectural choices, supply‑chain risk, cloud security, and digital identity. This breadth underpins sound recommendations across hybrid environments and OT networks.

Graph‑aware CTI operations
In OpenCTI, the agent leverages the STIX hypergraph to connect observables to campaigns, malware families, and threat actors, recommending pivots and adding sightings automatically. Stream connectors distribute updates in near real‑time to SIEMs, avoiding polling-heavy integrations.

Elastic/Splunk indicator ingestion and matching
By consuming OpenCTI’s indicator data streams, Elastic Security and Splunk can apply indicator match rules to generate alerts, while the agent correlates hits back to TTPs and NIST mitigations—closing the loop from detection to control action.

MISP export/import and TAXII interop
Teams can tag events for export, convert to STIX 2.x via misp‑stix, and publish over TAXII 2.1 to non‑MISP platforms. The agent respects tagging, filtering, and feed selection to prevent noisy distribution and to keep CTI consistent across tools.

Wazuh deep usage — Sysmon, ATT&CK, and rule tuning
With Sysmon telemetry and ATT&CK mapping in Wazuh, the agent recommends rule tuning and enrichment (e.g., registry, process creation) and links detections to mitigations and control requirements (SP 800‑53). This guidance accelerates threat hunting and reduces false positives.

On‑prem privacy & resilience
Because inference runs locally via GGUF, sensitive logs and indicators never leave your environment. Quantized variants allow CPU‑only operation—even in constrained ICS/OT segments—while maintaining workable throughput for SOC workflows.

Operational excellence — tokens/sec and deployment patterns
The model card documents performance and recommended quantizations; starting with Q4_K_M typically offers the best speed/quality ratio, with Q5_K_M/Q8_0 for higher fidelity. Deployments via Ollama or llama.cpp provide reproducible setups for dev, test, and prod.

Standards‑aligned remediation playbooks
For each finding, the agent maps mitigations to CSF functions and SP 800‑53 control families, proposes validation checks, and suggests reporting structures (e.g., artifacts for RMF authorization or CSF governance dashboards).  

Flexible integration choices
Whether you prefer OpenCTI live streams, TAXII collections, or direct CSV feeds, the platform supports multiple distribution methods so SOCs can choose between push, pull, and streaming models—reducing integration friction across existing tool stacks.

Connector ecosystem and future‑proofing
OpenCTI’s connector ecosystem continues to expand (Splunk, Elastic, Sentinel, QRadar), and community projects such as the opencti‑wazuh connector deepen bi‑directional use cases. Our agents are designed to slot into these flows without proprietary lock‑in.
